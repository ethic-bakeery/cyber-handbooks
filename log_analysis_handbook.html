Chapter 01

The Log Analysis Mindset

Before you touch a single log, you need to think right. The tool is secondary. The thinking is everything.

Most analysts open a SIEM, search for an alert, read some events, and write a ticket. That is log reading. It is not log analysis. Reading is passive. Analysis is deliberate and adversarial — you are reconstructing what happened by studying the traces left behind by someone who did not want to leave any. That distinction matters more than any query syntax you will ever learn.

This chapter is about getting your head right before your hands touch a keyboard. Every technique in this handbook depends on these principles. Skip them and you will be a fast, competent operator who still misses the thing that matters.

Reactive vs. Proactive: Two Different Jobs

Reactive analysis is what most people do: an alert fires, you investigate it, you close it. This is necessary, but it is the floor, not the ceiling. The problem is that alerts only fire on things someone anticipated. A well-crafted APT intrusion will not trigger your off-the-shelf rules. It will not generate a high-fidelity alert. It will be the quiet pattern in the background that the analyst who is actively looking for it would catch, and the analyst chasing tickets would miss entirely.

Proactive threat hunting is asking: if an attacker were in my environment right now, what would I expect to see? Then going to look for it without an alert prompting you. You are forming hypotheses based on threat intelligence and your knowledge of attacker behavior, then testing those hypotheses against your log data. When you find nothing, that is also data. When you find something, you just caught what automated detection missed.

🧠 Mindset

The moment you start hunting with a hypothesis rather than reacting to an alert, your detection capability multiplies. Form the hypothesis first: "I believe an attacker could be using scheduled tasks for persistence." Then go find evidence for or against it. This is the difference between waiting to be told something is wrong and knowing before anyone tells you.

Baseline Before Anomaly: You Cannot Spot Weird Without Knowing Normal

This is the single most important principle in this entire handbook. Write it on a sticky note and put it on your monitor. An event is only suspicious in context. PowerShell launching from a Word document is suspicious. PowerShell launching from a management workstation during business hours by an IT admin is routine. The event is identical. The context makes the determination.

Baselining means answering questions like: how many failed logons does this user normally generate per day? What processes normally run on this host? What does normal outbound traffic volume look like for this subnet? What DNS domains does this machine typically query? When you know normal, anything that deviates is a question worth asking. Without that baseline, you are drowning in events with no way to distinguish the ocean from the fish.

Thinking Like the Attacker

This sounds like a cliche, but it is the most practical habit you can build. Before you analyze a log, ask yourself: if I were trying to do what this alert suggests, what else would I have had to do first? What would I have to do next? What traces would I leave that I would want to clean up? What legitimate tools would I use to avoid detection?

A threat actor doing credential dumping with Mimikatz needs to have gotten onto the system first. They needed to authenticate somehow. They needed to run a process. They may have needed to disable antivirus or dump from a process they injected into. Every one of those steps left a log. You are not looking for the Mimikatz event in isolation — you are looking for the whole chain, and Mimikatz might be the least subtle part of it.

🎯 Real World

During an actual ransomware investigation, the initial entry point was a phishing email opened 23 days before encryption started. The attacker sat quietly doing reconnaissance and credential harvesting for over three weeks. The log evidence was there the entire time, buried under millions of normal events. When we worked backward from the encryption event, the phishing delivery event was right there on day one. The issue wasn't missing logs. It was nobody was looking for what they would have needed to find it.

The Signal-to-Noise Problem

A medium-sized enterprise with 2,000 endpoints running Sysmon, Windows Security logging, network logs, and DNS will generate somewhere between 50 and 200 million log events per day. The number of those events that represent attacker activity during a typical intrusion is maybe a few thousand. That is less than 0.002% of the total volume. Your job is to find those events without missing them and without drowning in the other 99.998%.

The way you do this is not by reading every log. It is by building filters that eliminate known-good, by focusing on high-value event types first, and by following threads rather than scanning randomly. You start with the events that are almost always meaningful — Event ID 1102 (log cleared), Sysmon Event 8 (CreateRemoteThread), 4625 failures followed by a 4624 success — and let those pull you toward the attacker's footprint. The noise doesn't go away. You just learn to focus past it.

The Pivot Mindset: Every Finding is a Question

This is the core habit of the threat hunter. You find a suspicious process creation event. The wrong response is to document it and close the ticket. The right response is to immediately ask three questions: who else on this host did this process interact with? Where else in the environment did this same binary run? What network connections did this process make?

Every indicator you extract from a finding is a pivot point. A username pivots to all activity by that user across all systems. A source IP pivots to everything that IP touched. A file hash pivots to every system that ran that binary. A command line pivots to similar commands across the environment. You are building a graph of attacker activity, and each pivot adds more nodes and edges to that graph until the full picture becomes visible.

Time: Your Best Friend and Worst Enemy

Timestamps are the backbone of log correlation. They are also one of the most manipulated artifacts in forensics. Three things to know cold before you analyze a single log:

First: All your log sources should be in UTC. If they are not, document the offsets and convert them before correlating. A log showing an event at 14:32 local time versus 19:32 UTC is not a discrepancy — it is a timezone. But if you're correlating Windows events from a system in UTC+0 with firewall logs from a device in UTC-5 and you don't account for it, you will place events five hours apart that actually happened simultaneously.

Second: Clock skew is real and common. Network devices, servers running without NTP, virtual machines that have been paused and resumed — they all accumulate clock drift. A 47-second clock skew on a host means events from that host appear 47 seconds later in time than they actually occurred. When you're correlating a connection event on a firewall with a process creation event on an endpoint, a 47-second gap might look like a legitimate delay when it's actually two events that happened at the same instant.

Third: Attackers tamper with timestamps. Timestomping — modifying file metadata to show a different creation or modification time — is a standard evasion technique. A malicious binary planted in System32 with a creation timestamp of 2019-04-15 looks like a legitimate Windows file. Check the MFT ($MFT) entry timestamps against the file system timestamps. If they don't match, something changed that timestamp after the fact.

Why Attackers Change TTPs and How to Hunt Behavior, Not Signatures

Signature-based detection works until the attacker changes their tool. Change the hash of the malware, and the hash-based detection is useless. Change the C2 domain, and the domain-based block is useless. Sophisticated threat actors — and even commodity actors now — rotate their infrastructure, retool their malware, and change the specific commands they run specifically to bypass the detection rules that caught them or caught their peers last time.

Behavioral detection doesn't care about the specific hash or the specific domain. It cares about what happened. PowerShell making an outbound HTTPS connection shortly after being spawned by winword.exe is suspicious regardless of which specific PowerShell command was run or which specific IP address was contacted. The behavior is the detection. Build your rules and hunts around behavior patterns — parent-child relationships, process lineage, sequence of events, statistical patterns — and you become much harder to evade.

Correlation: One Log Tells You Nothing

This is the reality of log analysis that takes analysts years to fully internalize. A single logon event proves someone authenticated. It doesn't prove anything malicious. A single process creation event proves a process ran. It doesn't prove the process was malicious. Even a Mimikatz detection by itself only proves Mimikatz ran — not what happened before it, what credentials were stolen, or where the attacker went next.

Correlation is taking the logon event, the process creation event, the network connection event, the DNS query, and the file creation event and showing they are all connected to the same attacker action at the same time on the same or connected systems. That combination is the evidence. Any one of those events alone is a question. All of them together is an answer.

🔥 Critical

Never write a conclusion based on a single log source. Never close an investigation because you couldn't find corroborating evidence in one source — look in another. The attacker chose the path of least detection. Your evidence might be in the DNS logs when the process logs are clean, or in the firewall logs when the endpoint logs were cleared. Keep pulling threads until you either confirm or definitively rule out. Definitively means more than one source agreeing.

Avoiding Tunnel Vision

This happens to every analyst. You start investigating one thing, you find something interesting, you keep digging into that one thread, and two hours later you've built a complete picture of one part of the attack while three other parts of the attack were happening in plain sight in other log sources. Meanwhile the attacker finished their objective.

The discipline is to constantly step back and ask: what am I not looking at right now? Every 20 minutes during an active incident, pull your eyes off the current thread and look at the big picture. Is there anomalous activity anywhere else? Is the SIEM generating alerts you haven't read yet? Is the network team seeing anything unusual? The attacker is not constrained to the one host you're staring at. Your investigation shouldn't be either.

Chapter 02

Log Sources, Formats & the Data Landscape

Every log source has a story. Know what each one can tell you — and what it cannot.

The most common failure in log analysis is not a query mistake. It is not knowing where to look. An analyst who only reads Windows Security logs during a Linux compromise will miss everything. An analyst who ignores DNS logs will never catch DNS tunneling. This chapter maps the entire terrain — every log source, every format, and what each one is good for detecting.

Windows Event Logs

Windows logs to .evtx binary files stored in C:\Windows\System32\winevt\Logs\. Each log channel is a separate file. The Security channel is gold — it holds authentication, privilege use, object access, and policy change events. The System channel tracks hardware, driver, and service events. Application logs application-specific events. But the channels you often need to explicitly enable and that most attackers don't expect you to have are the operational logs: PowerShell/Operational, Sysmon/Operational, TaskScheduler/Operational, WMI-Activity/Operational.

Event ID 4688 — Process Creation (The Most Valuable Event)

If you could only have one Windows event type enabled, it would be 4688 with command line logging enabled (disabled by default — you must turn it on via Group Policy). The combination of NewProcessName (what ran), ParentProcessName (what launched it), and CommandLine (with what arguments) is the process creation trinity. macOS Unified Logging System

Modern macOS moved to a binary logging framework called the Unified Logging System. Logs are stored in /var/db/diagnostics/ as binary .tracev3 files. You cannot read them directly with a text editor. You use log show or the Console app. The upside is the logs are structured, compressed, and fast to query. The downside is that many traditional log analysis tools can't read them without conversion.

💡 Tip

Key macOS subsystems for security: com.apple.loginwindow (authentication and session events), com.apple.securityd (keychain access, crypto operations), com.apple.opendirectoryd (directory service/LDAP authentication), com.apple.xpc.launchd (persistence via launch agents/daemons).

Network and Infrastructure Logs

Firewall Logs

Firewall logs are your network perimeter view. They show you what tried to go where and whether it was allowed. The fields that matter most are src_ip, dst_ip, dst_port, action (allow/deny), and bytes_in/bytes_out. Large bytes_out to an unknown external IP at an unusual hour is data exfiltration until proven otherwise. Regular short-duration connections to the same external IP with consistent byte counts is beaconing.

DNS Logs

DNS logs are underrated to the point of malpractice. Every piece of malware that communicates with the internet makes DNS queries. C2 domains, DGA (Domain Generation Algorithm) domains, DNS tunneling — it all shows up in DNS logs. If you don't have DNS logging enabled and centralized, you are blind to an enormous class of threats. Enable it. Collect it. Query it constantly.

Chapter 03

SIEM Tools & Query Languages

SPL, KQL, AQL, ES|QL — different syntax, same goal: make the data tell the truth.

The query language is the scalpel. A surgeon who doesn't know how to use a scalpel is not a surgeon. An analyst who can't write a SIEM query from scratch — who depends on saved searches and dashboards built by someone else — is not an analyst. They're a dashboard reader. This chapter makes you a query writer.

Splunk SPL — Search Processing Language

SPL is a pipeline language. You start with a data source and pipe it through a series of transformations. The mental model is Unix pipes: each command takes the output of the previous command and does something to it. The pipe character | chains commands. Read it left to right: fetch data, then filter, then transform, then aggregate, then display.

Core SPL Concepts

Every Splunk search starts with a source specification. index is which data store to search. sourcetype is the format/parser to use. source is the specific log file. You narrow the data first, then operate on it. Searching without narrowing the index is how analysts make their Splunk admins angry.

Microsoft Sentinel — KQL (Kusto Query Language)

KQL is a read-only query language designed for analytics at scale. Unlike SPL, KQL doesn't use pipes as the only operator — it uses a combination of operators (where, project, extend, summarize, join) that feel more like SQL for those with database backgrounds. The key mental model is: table name first, then filter down, then transform.

KQL Table Ecosystem for Security

In Sentinel, data lives in tables. You need to know which table holds what. SecurityEvent is Windows Event Log. SigninLogs is Azure AD sign-ins. DeviceProcessEvents is MDE process telemetry. CommonSecurityLog is CEF-formatted data from firewalls and network devices. DnsEvents is DNS query logs. Know these tables cold.

KQL / Sentinel Beaconing Detection — Regular Interval Connections to Same IP
⌕
let timeframe = ago(24h); let min_connections = 20; CommonSecurityLog | where TimeGenerated > timeframe | where DeviceAction == "allow" | summarize connection_times = make_list(TimeGenerated), count = count() by SourceIP, DestinationIP, DestinationPort | where count > min_connections | extend intervals = array_sort_asc(connection_times) | extend jitter_stddev = stdev(array_length(intervals)) | where jitter_stddev < 30 // Very regular = low jitter = beacon | project SourceIP, DestinationIP, DestinationPort, count, jitter_stddev | sort by jitter_stddev asc

Credential Access — LSASS Dumping

LSASS (Local Security Authority Subsystem Service) holds credential material in memory — NTLM hashes, Kerberos tickets, sometimes cleartext passwords. Tools like Mimikatz, ProcDump, and custom malware access LSASS memory to extract these credentials. Sysmon Event 10 (ProcessAccess) captures this — but only if you are collecting it, and only if you filter on the right access rights.

The GrantedAccess field in Sysmon 10 is the key. Legitimate processes that access LSASS (Windows Defender, csrss) use specific minimal access rights. Credential dumpers request PROCESS_VM_READ (0x0010) plus PROCESS_QUERY_INFORMATION (0x0400), which combined gives 0x1010. Mimikatz specifically requests 0x1410. These values are your detection.

Credential Access — Kerberoasting

Kerberoasting is an offline attack. The attacker requests service tickets for accounts with Service Principal Names (SPNs) set. They request those tickets encrypted with RC4-HMAC (encryption type 0x17) rather than AES256 (0x12), because RC4 hashes are crackable offline much faster than AES. The requests look like legitimate Kerberos authentication — because they are. The only anomaly is the encryption type choice and the volume of requests from a single source.

Exfiltration — DNS Tunneling

DNS tunneling encodes data into DNS queries. The compromised host sends queries to a domain controlled by the attacker, with the actual data encoded in the subdomain label. The attacker's DNS server decodes it. Responses carry data back. It bypasses most firewalls because DNS is almost never blocked outbound.

Detection relies on identifying the statistical abnormality. Legitimate DNS queries have short subdomains (the hostname), low volume to any single domain, and consistent record types (A, AAAA, MX). DNS tunneling produces long random-looking subdomains (the encoded data), high query volume to one domain, and often unusual record types (TXT, NULL) used for larger payloads.

Advanced Techniques

Entropy analysis, beaconing math, behavioral baselines, and graph-based thinking — the difference between good and elite.

This chapter covers techniques that separate analysts who find commodity malware from analysts who find APTs. Commodity malware tends to be noisy — it triggers signatures and makes obvious connections. APTs are quiet. They use legitimate tools, maintain long-term low-and-slow access, and only make brief connections for necessary operations. Finding them requires statistical thinking, not just pattern matching.

Shannon Entropy — Detecting Encoded/Random Strings

Entropy measures randomness in a string. High entropy means the string is close to random — which is characteristic of base64-encoded data, encrypted content, or randomly generated domain names (DGA). Legitimate process names, domain names, and file names are low-entropy because they are pronounceable human-chosen words. A process named svchost.exe has low entropy. A process named k8Hd9xQ2mPn7.exe has high entropy. That difference is detectable algorithmically.

Python — Shannon Entropy Calculator
Calculate entropy of strings to detect DGA domains, encoded commands, and random binary names
import math
from collections import Counter

def entropy(s):
    s = s.lower()
    counts = Counter(s)
    length = len(s)
    return -sum((c/length) * math.log2(c/length) for c in counts.values())

domains = [
    "google.com",
    "microsoft.com",

    "k8Hd9xQ2mPn7LwRs",              # Random binary name
]

for d in domains:
    e = entropy(d)
    flag = "INVESTIGATE" if e > 3.5 else "normal"
    print(f"{e:.2f}  [{flag}]  {d}")

Long-Tail Frequency Analysis

The long-tail principle in threat hunting: in any large dataset, the most common items occupy most of the volume, and the rare items are at the tail. Attackers hide in the tail. Their tools run once. Their C2 domains appear in one or two DNS queries. Their malicious scheduled task is the one task that has never run before. The frequency analysis method is your systematic way to find the tail.

🧠 Mindset — The Long Tail Hunt

When you build a frequency distribution of any event type and look at the bottom of the list, you are looking for things that happen 1-3 times. Ask: is there a legitimate reason this happened only once? An admin tool that runs once could be a legitimate maintenance task — or it could be an attacker's payload that ran once to establish persistence and will not run again until reboot. The context of what the binary IS determines whether one execution is normal. For unusual binaries in unusual paths, once is enough to investigate.

Timestamp Forensics — Detecting Log Tampering

Sophisticated attackers tamper with log timestamps. The way to detect this is to look for consistency across multiple time-tracking mechanisms. Windows maintains multiple timestamps for files: the $STANDARD_INFORMATION attribute (what you see in Explorer, easily modified) and the $FILE_NAME attribute in the MFT (harder to modify). If these disagree significantly, someone changed the timestamps. Additionally, the PE compile timestamp embedded in the binary itself is a third data point — though it can be falsified too, most malware authors don't bother.

💀 Trap

Do not trust a binary's timestamps to tell you when it was placed on a system. Timestomping — using tools like Metasploit's timestomp or PowerShell's SetCreationTime/SetLastWriteTime — trivially changes $STANDARD_INFORMATION timestamps to match legitimate Windows files. Always check the MFT $FILE_NAME timestamps using forensic tools like MFTECmd or Velociraptor, and compare against the $STANDARD_INFORMATION entry. Discrepancies between the two are strong indicators of anti-forensics.

The Analyst Playbook

Three complete investigation workflows — from first alert to full root cause.

A playbook is not a rigid script. It is a thinking framework with decision points. Real incidents never follow the exact path the playbook describes. But having the framework means you never stare at the screen wondering what to do next. You always have a next step.

Playbook 1: Ransomware Incident

🗺️ Ransomware Investigation Decision Tree

The most important thing in a ransomware investigation is establishing the dwell time — how long was the attacker in your environment before encryption started? The average ransomware dwell time is measured in days to weeks. During that window, they were doing reconnaissance, stealing credentials, and exfiltrating data. The encryption event is the very last thing they did, and it is usually designed to force your hand. The real damage — data theft, credential compromise, backdoor planting — happened earlier and quietly.

🔥 Critical — Ransomware Triage Order

First: check if data was exfiltrated before encryption (check large outbound transfers in the 7-30 days before the encryption event). Second: identify patient zero (the first host that executed the ransomware loader — that is your initial access point). Third: identify which accounts were compromised during the intrusion (rotate ALL domain credentials, not just the obviously compromised ones). Fourth: check for persistent backdoors installed during the dwell period — removing ransomware without removing the backdoor means re-encryption in two weeks.

Playbook 2: Insider Threat Investigation

Insider threat investigations are the most legally sensitive investigations you will run. The difference between an employee doing their job and an employee stealing data is often subtle. Your analysis must be thorough, documented, and defensible. "Defensible" means: you have the raw evidence, you can explain exactly what it shows, and you have not made assumptions that go beyond what the evidence supports.

The starting point is usually an anomaly: unusually large data access, access to systems outside normal job function, access at unusual hours, or large uploads to personal cloud storage. Each of these is a starting hypothesis, not a conclusion. Your investigation tests the hypothesis.

Chapter 11

Analyst Traps & False Positives

The mistakes that cost investigations hours — and sometimes let attackers win.

Every analyst makes these mistakes at some point. The goal is to make them once, recognize them, and build habits that prevent them from happening again. The traps in this chapter are not theoretical — they are patterns that have directly caused missed detections in real incidents.

💀 Trap 1 — Assuming the First Suspicious Thing You Find is Patient Zero

The first alert that fires is usually not the beginning of the incident. It is the first thing that got loud enough to trigger a detection rule. A credential dump event at 14:31 is suspicious. But the attacker was in your environment since 09:17 the same morning — or three weeks ago. Always work backward from your first confirmed event. The actual entry point is usually earlier and quieter.

💀 Trap 2 — Trusting a Clean VirusTotal Result

VirusTotal checks a file hash against antivirus vendor signatures. A clean VT result means that specific hash has not been seen by those vendors with a malicious classification. It does not mean the file is safe. Custom malware, freshly compiled payloads, and legitimate tools used maliciously will all show clean on VT. VirusTotal is useful for confirming something is known-bad. It is useless for confirming something is known-good. Use behavioral analysis — what did the binary DO? — not just hash reputation.

💀 Trap 3 — Treating All 4625 Events as Brute Force

Windows Event 4625 fires for every authentication failure. The vast majority of 4625 events in any enterprise environment are service accounts with stale passwords, users mistyping their PIN, and automated processes hitting the wrong credentials. Calling all 4625 volume "brute force" generates endless false positives and alert fatigue. The actual brute force indicator is not the volume of 4625s alone — it is the volume per source IP against a specific account, combined with the Status codes (0xC000006A = valid username, wrong password), combined with whether a success followed.

💀 Trap 4 — Missing the Successful Logon After a String of Failures

This is the reverse of Trap 3. An analyst builds a query for 4625 events, finds a spike, writes a note "possible brute force," and moves on. They never check whether any of the targeted accounts had a corresponding 4624 success event from the same source within the same time window. The 4625 events are the attempt. The 4624 is the compromise. Always correlate the failure events with success events from the same source.

💀 Trap 5 — Clock Skew Attribution Errors

You pull logs from a workstation and a domain controller and try to correlate them. The workstation shows an event at 14:47:22. The DC shows what should be the corresponding event at 14:52:08 — a 5-minute gap that makes the events look unrelated. In reality, the workstation's clock drifted 5 minutes. The events are simultaneous. Always check whether your log sources are NTP-synced and document any known clock skew before correlating across sources. A 5-minute skew on an endpoint is not unusual, especially on endpoints that have been offline.

💀 Trap 6 — Assuming Lateral Movement Always Creates 4624 Events

Token impersonation techniques — like those used in Potato attacks (PrintSpoofer, JuicyPotato) — can impersonate system tokens without creating a new logon event in the Security log. If an attacker impersonates the SYSTEM token from a service account shell, they operate with SYSTEM privileges but you will not see a corresponding 4624. Sysmon Event 7 (image load) showing specific DLLs associated with token manipulation and process lineage analysis are more reliable than waiting for a 4624 to appear.

💀 Trap 7 — Tunnel Vision: Staring at One Source While the Attack Expands

You find Mimikatz activity on WKSTN-047. You spend 90 minutes doing deep forensic analysis of WKSTN-047. Meanwhile, the attacker used the credentials they dumped to log into the file server, the backup server, and the domain controller. You are building the perfect evidence package for one node while the attacker completes their objective on three others. During active incidents, do broad scoping first. Deep forensics on a single host comes after you have mapped the full scope of the intrusion.

💀 Trap 8 — Deliberate Noise Generation

Some sophisticated threat actors deliberately generate high-volume noisy activity — mass login failures against a honeypot account, suspicious but harmless process executions — specifically to overwhelm the SOC and bury their actual activity in the noise. If you suddenly see a massive spike in obviously suspicious but oddly harmless activity, consider that you may be being distracted. Pull your eyes off the noisy events and look at what quiet, low-volume activity is happening simultaneously on other hosts.

Chapter 12

Building Your Log Analysis Lab

Practice on real attack data before the real attack happens to you.

Reading this handbook is the start, not the finish. The techniques here become instinctive only through practice. The best way to practice is to generate real attack log data and analyze it yourself. This chapter tells you exactly how to build that environment for free.

The Stack

The minimum viable lab for Windows log analysis practice consists of four components: a Windows VM to generate events, Sysmon for enriched telemetry, Elastic SIEM (free tier) for log storage and querying, and Atomic Red Team to simulate attacker techniques and generate realistic log data to analyze.

💡 Sysmon Config

Use the olafhartong modular Sysmon configuration from GitHub as your starting point. It is maintained by the community, balances coverage with noise reduction, and is what most mature SOCs base their Sysmon deployment on. Install Sysmon with: Sysmon64.exe -accepteula -i sysmonconfig.xml. Events appear in Microsoft-Windows-Sysmon/Operational.

💡 Atomic Red Team

Atomic Red Team is a library of small, contained test cases that simulate specific ATT&CK techniques. Each "atomic test" generates the exact log artifacts that the real technique would generate. Use it to generate known-malicious activity, then practice finding that activity in your SIEM. Start with the techniques covered in Chapter 6 — run each one, then try to detect it using the queries in this handbook.

Free Resources

  • EVTX attack samples: The "EVTX-ATTACK-SAMPLES" GitHub repository by sbousseaden contains pre-collected .evtx files from simulated attacks. Download them and practice querying without needing to set up your own lab first.
  • Splunk Attack Range: Splunk's free lab environment that automatically spins up a complete attack simulation environment in AWS or locally using Vagrant. It generates Windows, Linux, and network logs simultaneously.
  • Microsoft Sentinel Free Trial: 31-day free trial with a free workspace tier. Ingest up to 10GB/day for free for 31 days. Enough to practice all KQL queries in this handbook.
  • Elastic Security Free: The detection engine is free in the basic tier. Run it against your lab logs to practice building Elastic detection rules.
  • SANS Holiday Hack Challenge: Annual challenge with realistic log analysis scenarios. Archived challenges are available year-round. These are professionally designed and cover exactly the techniques in this handbook.

Practice Workflow

The practice workflow that accelerates skill development fastest is: pick a specific ATT&CK technique, read how it works, run the Atomic Red Team test for it, then try to find the evidence in your SIEM using only the techniques from this handbook. Do not look at the answer first. Force yourself to build the query from first principles. If you find it, build a detection rule for it. If you cannot find it, figure out what log source or configuration you are missing. That gap is your next lab task.

🎯 Real World — The 30-Day Lab Challenge

Here is the challenge that has produced more competent analysts than any training course: run one Atomic Red Team test per day for 30 days. Detect it in your SIEM. Document how you found it, what the detection query is, and what it would look like evaded. After 30 days, you will have 30 detection techniques you understand end to end, 30 SIEM queries you built yourself, and the mental muscle memory to recognize those artifacts instantly in real incidents. This is how you go from analyst who reads alerts to analyst who hunts threats.

Final Word

Log analysis is a craft. It takes time to develop the instincts, and those instincts come from pattern exposure — seeing thousands of events, recognizing what normal looks like, and developing the alarm bell in your head that fires when something is off. No handbook can give you those instincts directly. What this handbook can do is compress the conceptual framework and give you the language and tools to start building that experience faster.

The most important thing I can tell you after everything in these chapters is this: the attacker is patient and deliberate. They study your environment before moving. They minimize their footprint. They use tools you trust. Your advantage is that they cannot erase every trace, and log data is honest. It does not lie, it does not get confused, and it does not forget. Everything they did left a mark somewhere. Your job is to find it before they find the data they came for.

Keep querying. Keep hunting. Keep updating your knowledge of attacker techniques — they update theirs constantly, and the moment you stop learning is the moment you start missing things. The best analysts I know are still learning after 20 years. That is not a sign of weakness. That is what makes them the best.
