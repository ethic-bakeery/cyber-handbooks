Chapter 01 — Foundations

The Forensic Investigator's Mindset

Before you touch a single log file or run a single command, you need to understand how forensic investigators think. The tools change every year. The mindset doesn't.

Reactive vs. Hypothesis-Driven Investigation

Most analysts start their careers as reactive investigators. An alert fires, they look at the alert, they follow the alert's breadcrumbs, and they write up what the alert told them. This works — until it doesn't. Sophisticated attackers know exactly which alerts they'll trigger and engineer around them. When your investigation strategy is "follow the alerts," an attacker who avoids triggering alerts is effectively invisible to you.

Hypothesis-driven investigation works differently. You start not with an alert but with a question: "If an attacker had compromised this system, what would they have done?" You form a hypothesis — say, "the attacker established persistence via a scheduled task" — and then you go looking for evidence that would confirm or refute that hypothesis. You know in advance what confirming evidence looks like (a new task created outside business hours, task pointing to a temp directory, task using encoded PowerShell). You know what refuting evidence looks like (no tasks created in the relevant time window, or tasks whose ImagePath checks out as legitimate). Either way, you're searching purposefully rather than randomly.

The critical advantage is that hypothesis-driven analysis is complete. You're not just documenting what happened — you're actively disproving alternative explanations. When you write your incident report saying "the attacker used scheduled task persistence," you can say with confidence that you checked for WMI subscriptions, Run key persistence, service installation, and DLL hijacking, and none of those were present. That's the difference between a finding and a conclusion.

🧠 Analyst Mindset
The question is never "what does this log show me?" The question is always "if my hypothesis is correct, what would this log contain? If it's wrong, what would be missing?" A log that shows nothing is evidence too — it means whatever you were looking for didn't happen here.

The Four-Phase Investigation Loop

Forensic investigation is not a linear process. It's an iterative loop, and understanding that loop is what separates analysts who solve cases from analysts who catalog artifacts. The four phases are: gather context, form hypothesis, identify discriminating evidence, then analyze and revise. You run this loop repeatedly, and each iteration narrows the possibility space.

Phase 1 — Context gathering means understanding what you're walking into before you analyze anything. What is this system? What does it do? Who uses it? What are the normal processes, normal network connections, normal logon hours? Without this baseline, you cannot identify anomalies. A PowerShell process spawned by Word is always suspicious. A PowerShell process on an IT administrator's workstation at 2pm on a Tuesday may be completely routine. Context determines everything.

Phase 2 — Hypothesis formation is where you commit to a specific theory about what happened. "This system was compromised through a phishing email, the attacker established persistence, and exfiltrated data" is a hypothesis. It's specific enough to be falsifiable. "Something bad happened" is not a hypothesis. Good hypotheses are informed by threat intelligence — what are attackers in your industry actually doing right now? They're informed by the initial alert or tip that triggered the investigation. And they're informed by the attacker's likely objective: why would anyone want access to this specific system?

Phase 3 — Evidence identification means listing, before you search, what evidence would confirm your hypothesis and what evidence would refute it. If you think the attacker used a phishing email for initial access, confirming evidence includes email delivery logs showing an attachment sent to the user, browser download records, a file in the Downloads folder matching the email attachment, and process creation events showing that file executing. Refuting evidence includes no email matching the timeframe, no download records, no execution artifacts. Write the list before you search — it prevents confirmation bias.

Phase 4 — Analyze and revise means honestly evaluating what you found against what you expected. If the evidence mostly confirms your hypothesis, your confidence increases and you move to the next phase of the attack. If the evidence partially confirms it, you refine the hypothesis. If the evidence refutes it, you discard the hypothesis and form a new one. The trap here is refusing to revise — analysts who are emotionally invested in their theory will unconsciously ignore refuting evidence. Stay detached from your hypothesis. It's just a tool for directing your search, not a conclusion you're defending.

The four-phase investigation loop. This is not linear — each cycle tightens the hypothesis until you reach a defensible conclusion.

The Importance of Baselining

You cannot identify anomalies without knowing normal. This sounds obvious, but most analysts skip the baselining step because it feels tedious — they want to jump straight to the "interesting" artifacts. That's backwards. Without a baseline, you'll spend hours investigating legitimate activity and miss actual malicious behavior because it blends into the noise you haven't measured.

Consider this scenario: during an investigation, you observe that PowerShell.exe was executed with the argument -ExecutionPolicy Bypass -File C:\Users\jsmith\AppData\Local\Temp\update.ps1. Is this malicious? You cannot answer that question without context. On a standard employee workstation with no PowerShell usage baseline, executing any script from AppData\Local\Temp is deeply suspicious — that's a common malware staging location, and bypassing execution policy suggests the script wasn't signed. On a desktop engineer's workstation where they're known to test deployment scripts, you need to know whether this specific script was part of a known-good deployment workflow. Exact same event. Completely different forensic significance.

⭐ Key Principle
Build your baseline from multiple independent sources. For any given system, establish: what processes normally run (from Sysmon or EDR historical data), what outbound network connections are normal (from DNS/proxy logs), what accounts normally authenticate (from Security log), and what the normal logon hours are. Any of these individually can be faked; all four together are much harder to fake consistently.

The practical approach is to look at 30-day rolling statistics for the system in question before you start analyzing specific events. What was the average number of process creation events per day? What external IPs does this system regularly talk to? What's the typical count of authentication events? When you're doing this in the middle of an incident, you often won't have perfect baselines — but even a rough baseline is better than none. And when you're building detection engineering outside an active incident, establishing formal baselines for your critical asset classes is one of the highest-value things you can do.

Attacker Goal Mapping

Every attacker — from a script kiddie running a commodity RAT to a nation-state APT with custom zero-days — must accomplish the same high-level objectives to achieve their goal. The Mitre ATT&CK framework documents these as Tactics, and they're universal because they're not about the tools used, they're about the physical reality of how computer intrusions work. Initial access, execution, persistence, privilege escalation, defense evasion, credential access, discovery, lateral movement, collection, exfiltration, impact. Every intrusion hits a subset of these in sequence.

Why does this matter for forensics? Because each tactic generates forensic artifacts. When you know what tactic the attacker was pursuing at a given stage, you know what artifacts to look for — even if the attacker tried to clean up. An attacker who wanted persistence had to either modify the registry, create a scheduled task, install a service, add a cron job, drop a LaunchAgent, or use another persistence mechanism. They couldn't achieve persistence without leaving a trace in at least one of those locations. The tactic constrains the artifact space.

This means that when you have a partial timeline — say, you know initial access happened on Tuesday at 14:32 based on an email gateway log — you can make predictions about what should have happened next. Within minutes of initial access, the attacker almost certainly ran discovery commands (ipconfig, whoami, net group, systeminfo). Within hours, they probably attempted lateral movement. If you look for those artifacts and don't find them, either your timeline is wrong, or the attacker was very disciplined and very lucky at cleaning up. Both possibilities generate new hypothesis threads worth investigating.

🎯 Real World Example
During a 2023 ransomware investigation, the team initially couldn't find the initial access vector — no phishing email in the logs, no exploited service in the firewall logs. But by reasoning backwards from the persistence artifacts found (a new service installed at 02:14 on a Saturday), they hypothesized that the initial access must have occurred before that timestamp. They searched ProxyLogon vulnerability logs against their Exchange server and found the exploitation attempt 4 hours earlier — an entry that had been overlooked because the responders were focused on the endpoint, not the email infrastructure. Attacker goal mapping told them where to look.

Anti-Forensics Awareness

Anti-forensics is the attacker's attempt to hide evidence. It's important to understand, and more important to not be intimidated by. The foundational insight is this: every anti-forensics action itself generates a forensic artifact. Clearing the Security event log creates Event ID 1102. Wiping a file leaves patterns in unallocated space. Timestomping creates timestamp inconsistencies across multiple metadata sources. The attacker may hide the direct evidence of their activity, but they create new evidence of their hiding activity.

Evidence Handling & Chain of Custody

Digital evidence is like a snowflake — touch it wrong and it changes forever. Forensic procedure exists to make your evidence defensible in any context, not just legally. A technically correct finding presented on improperly acquired evidence can destroy an entire case.

Evidence Volatility and the Order of Collection

Not all evidence is created equal. Some evidence exists only for seconds — the current state of CPU registers. Some persists for hours — active network connections. Some lasts until the next reboot — running processes and open file handles. Some survives indefinitely — the contents of a hard drive. The Order of Volatility, first formalized in RFC 3227, defines the sequence in which you should collect evidence based on how quickly it disappears. The rule is simple: always collect the most volatile evidence first, because by the time you get to it second, it may be gone.

Order of Volatility — always collect left to right. Pulling power destroys everything in the first three categories immediately.

The practical implication: when you walk up to a live, running system that you believe has been compromised, your first action should never be to shut it down. That instinct — "contain the threat by cutting its power" — is exactly wrong for preservation. Cutting power destroys RAM, which may be the only place where fileless malware exists, encryption keys are stored, and plaintext credentials are accessible. Instead, capture memory first, then document running processes and network connections, then proceed to disk acquisition while the system is running (live acquisition) or after a controlled shutdown.

Chain of Custody — From Detection to Analysis

Chain of custody is the documented proof that evidence has been handled properly, stored securely, and not modified since collection. It's not bureaucratic paperwork — it's the mechanism by which your technical findings carry weight in any consequential context. Without chain of custody documentation, an opposing party in litigation can challenge whether the evidence you're presenting is the same evidence you collected, or whether it was modified in transit. That challenge, even if unsuccessful, creates doubt.

Every transfer of evidence — physical or logical — requires documentation. When the first responder bags a hard drive, that's documented. When the drive is transferred to the forensics lab, that's documented. When a forensic analyst checks it out of evidence storage to perform analysis, that's documented. When the analyst creates a forensic image, both the original drive and the image are hashed and the hashes recorded. When the image is transferred to an analysis workstation, that's documented. Every person who touches the evidence must sign, date, and time their contact with it.

🔥 Critical — Never Work on Original Evidence
This is the most commonly violated rule in digital forensics. You must always create a forensic image of the original and work exclusively from verified copies. If you analyze the original drive, you modify it — access timestamps change, file system metadata updates, unallocated space can be overwritten by the OS. Working on original evidence is not just a procedure violation — it permanently and irreversibly alters the evidence you're trying to preserve.

Evidence Integrity via Cryptographic Hashing

The hash is your evidence's fingerprint. A SHA-256 hash is a 256-bit value deterministically derived from the content of a file. Change a single bit of the file — any bit, anywhere — and the hash changes completely. This means that if your forensic image has the same SHA-256 hash as the original drive, they are mathematically proven to be identical in content. Any modification, accidental or deliberate, would change the hash.

MD5 was the standard for decades but has known collision vulnerabilities — an attacker can theoretically craft two different files with the same MD5 hash. SHA-256 has no known practical collision attacks. Use SHA-256 for all new cases. Keep MD5 only for legacy compatibility with older cases that were documented with MD5 hashes.

The correct procedure is to hash the original device before acquisition, hash the forensic image immediately after acquisition, verify they match, and record both hashes in the chain of custody documentation. Re-verify the hash of your working copy before any major analysis phase. This takes under a minute for most images and costs nothing if hashes match.

Common Evidence Handling Mistakes

⚠️ Trap — Booting the Original Evidence Disk
If you boot the suspect system from its own disk, Windows will update hundreds of registry keys, modify dozens of file timestamps, write swap file entries, and potentially overwrite unallocated clusters with new data. A 5-second boot can destroy hours of forensic value. Use an external forensic boot drive (CAINE, SANS SIFT) that runs entirely from its own media without touching the evidence drive.
⚠️ Trap — Working on the Original Instead of a Copy
Even mounting a drive read-only via the OS is insufficient for forensic work — OS-level read-only protection can be bypassed, and some file systems update access timestamps on read. Always use a hardware write-blocker (Tableau, WiebeTech) between the evidence drive and your analysis system. Software write-blockers are a second-best option — hardware blockers operate at the physical layer and cannot be bypassed by the OS.
⚠️ Trap — Failing to Document Every Action
Everything you do on or with evidence must be documented in real time — not reconstructed from memory later. Your documentation should be complete enough that another analyst, given only your notes and the forensic image, could reproduce exactly what you did and reach the same findings. If you can't explain why you ran a particular command, you probably shouldn't have run it.
⚠️ Trap — Not Verifying Hash After Acquisition
Acquisition errors are more common than people expect. Media errors, transfer interruptions, insufficient write buffer, and destination drive issues all cause silent corruption. Always verify the hash of your image against the source immediately after acquisition, while both are still accessible. Finding a hash mismatch three weeks into analysis — when the original system has been rebuilt — means starting over with potentially unrecoverable evidence.
⚠️ Trap — Improper Storage
Digital evidence degrades physically. Hard drives stored improperly (humidity, temperature extremes, magnetic fields near speakers or motors) can lose data over months. Flash media has a finite retention period without power cycling. Optical media degrades. Store all evidence drives in static-shielded bags, at room temperature, away from magnetic fields. Maintain a documented storage access log for every evidence item.

Disk & File System Forensics

File systems don't just store files — they leave a rich archaeological record of everything that ever happened to them. Understanding how NTFS, EXT4, and APFS actually work at the metadata level turns disk forensics from artifact hunting into structured evidence recovery.

NTFS — The Forensic Analyst's Goldmine

NTFS is the most forensically rich file system in common use. This isn't an accident — it was designed with journaling, metadata redundancy, and change tracking features that primarily exist for reliability, but happen to be exactly what forensic analysts need. Understanding its internals is non-negotiable for any analyst working Windows environments.

The Master File Table ($MFT)

Every file and directory in an NTFS volume has an entry in the Master File Table. Think of the MFT as the volume's index — it contains not just filenames and pointers to data clusters, but full metadata records including all timestamps, security descriptors, file attributes, and for small files, the actual file data itself. The MFT entry for a file persists even after the file is deleted — only the "in use" flag is cleared. This means that the MFT is an index of every file that has ever existed on the volume, not just the ones currently present.

For forensic analysis, the MFT provides: accurate timestamps (both the easily-modified $STANDARD_INFORMATION timestamps and the harder-to-modify $FILE_NAME timestamps), evidence of deleted files before their MFT entries are reused, file size history, parent directory information, and for resident files (under about 700 bytes), the actual file content without needing to locate the data clusters. Parse the MFT with MFTECmd from Eric Zimmermann's toolkit — it produces a CSV output that covers every file, deleted or present, with complete metadata.

The Change Journal ($UsnJrnl)

The USN Journal is arguably the most valuable forensic artifact in the Windows ecosystem after event logs. It records every file create, modify, rename, move, and delete operation — with timestamps — for as long as the journal doesn't roll over (typically 32-64MB by default, but this covers weeks of activity on most systems). Crucially, the $UsnJrnl records these events even for files that have since been deleted. If an attacker downloaded malware to the Desktop, executed it, then deleted it, the $UsnJrnl will contain records of the file creation, the file execution (last access timestamp update), and the file deletion — all timestamped. You can reconstruct the file's complete lifecycle from birth to deletion.

$LogFile and Volume Shadow Copies

The $LogFile is NTFS's transaction journal — it records metadata changes to ensure the file system can recover from crashes. While it's not a forensic goldmine like the $UsnJrnl (it's circular and overwrites quickly), it can sometimes reveal MFT changes that happened before the USN journal coverage window. More importantly for forensic analysis, Volume Shadow Copies often contain older versions of the $MFT, $UsnJrnl, and $LogFile from earlier points in time — this means you may have access to file system metadata from weeks or months before the incident, allowing you to establish what the system looked like before the attacker arrived.

🧠 Analyst Mindset — Timestamp Comparison
Always compare all four timestamps from both locations for any file under examination. Normal files have $SI and $FN timestamps that are close together (within seconds — the time between file creation and MFT entry creation). A file where $SI Created is years older than $FN Created was definitively timestomped. A file where all timestamps are round numbers (midnight, January 1st, whole hours) was almost certainly timestomped with an automated tool. Real files are created at irregular, realistic times.

Deleted File Recovery

File deletion in NTFS is a three-step process: the MFT entry is marked "not in use" (the data is not zeroed), the clusters occupied by the file are marked available in the $Bitmap (but their content is not modified), and the directory entry referencing the file is removed. The actual file data on disk remains intact until new data overwrites those specific clusters. In practice, recently deleted files on systems with available disk space are recoverable with near certainty via metadata-based recovery. On full disks or after heavy write activity, file carving may be required.

Metadata-based recovery uses the surviving MFT entry (which still contains the filename, timestamps, and cluster allocation map) to locate and copy the data clusters. This works as long as the MFT entry hasn't been reused for a new file. Tools: Autopsy, FTK, Recuva, TestDisk.

File carving is signature-based recovery from raw disk data when metadata is gone. The carving tool scans unallocated space for known file header signatures (magic bytes), follows the file structure to find the end signature or estimates the file size, and extracts the raw bytes as a file. Carving recovers the data but not necessarily the filename or original timestamps.

NTFS Alternate Data Streams

NTFS Alternate Data Streams (ADS) allow multiple data streams to be attached to a single file. The primary stream is what you normally see when you open a file. Malware authors use ADS to hide payloads alongside innocent-looking files, exploiting the fact that the file's visible size and content don't hint at the hidden stream's existence.

EXT4 and APFS Forensic Characteristics

EXT4 uses inodes to store file metadata. Each file has exactly one inode, and the inode contains timestamps, permissions, link count, and pointers to data blocks. When a file is deleted in EXT4, the inode's data block pointers are zeroed (unlike NTFS, which doesn't touch the data clusters). This means EXT4 file carving requires signature-based recovery rather than metadata-based recovery for most deleted files. However, the inode itself often survives with the file's timestamps intact, which provides forensic evidence that the file existed even if the content is unrecoverable.

Inode reuse is an important EXT4 concept: after deletion, inodes and data blocks are returned to the free pool and will be reused for new files. On active systems with high churn, deleted files may be overwritten within minutes. The debugfs utility can examine free inodes and sometimes recover metadata from recently freed inodes before reuse.

APFS (Apple File System) uses copy-on-write semantics for all writes. When any data or metadata is modified, APFS writes the new version to a new location rather than overwriting the old version in place. This means old versions persist in unallocated space until the space is actively reclaimed, often providing better-than-expected forensic recovery compared to other file systems. APFS also maintains snapshots for Time Machine backups, which can provide forensic views of the file system at historical points in time.

Chapter 04 — Foundations

Memory Forensics

Memory forensics is the most powerful technique in modern DFIR — and the most underused. If you're not capturing and analyzing RAM on every significant incident, you're missing entire classes of evidence that exist nowhere else.

Fileless malware leaves nothing on disk. Encryption keys live in RAM. Unpacked malware payloads exist only in memory — antivirus sees the encrypted wrapper on disk and passes it; memory forensics sees the decrypted, executing code. Injected shellcode exists in the memory space of a legitimate process, invisible to any artifact-based detection. The credentials that enable lateral movement live in the LSASS process memory. The truth is in RAM, and it's available only while the system is running.

The reason most analysts underuse memory forensics is that it requires learning a new toolset (Volatility) and a new mental model (process trees, memory regions, kernel structures) on top of everything else. That investment pays massive dividends in detection capability. An analyst who can run a full memory triage in 30 minutes will solve incidents that analysts without that skill will never fully understand.

Memory Acquisition

Memory acquisition must happen before any other significant action on a live system. Every tool you run, every command you type, every file you open modifies memory — overwriting potentially critical evidence. For Windows, WinPmem and DumpIt are both reliable, require no installation (single executable), and produce standard raw memory images compatible with Volatility. For Linux, LiME (Linux Memory Extractor) is a kernel module that must be loaded and produces a raw dump via netcat or local file. For macOS, osxpmem works on Intel Macs — Apple Silicon acquisition is more complex due to secure boot constraints.

Volatility 3 Investigation Sequence

Volatility 3 is the modern version of the Volatility memory forensics framework. It dropped the old profile system (which required knowing the exact OS version and finding a matching symbol table) in favor of automated symbol resolution using Microsoft's public symbol server. For most Windows incidents, Volatility 3 will automatically identify the OS and load appropriate symbols. The analysis sequence below is the investigative order that an experienced analyst follows — not a random list of plugins, but a structured investigation that uses each finding to direct the next query.

1
OS Identification and Basic Enumeration
Start with windows.info to confirm the OS version and architecture, then windows.pslist for the basic running process list. This establishes your baseline before you start looking for anomalies. Note the total process count — on a standard workstation, expect 50-80 processes. Significantly more may indicate process injection spawning dozens of instances.
2
Process Tree Analysis — Know Normal to Find Abnormal
Run windows.pstree to see the parent-child relationships. Know the expected parent-child pairs: System spawns smss.exe; smss.exe spawns wininit.exe and winlogon.exe; wininit.exe spawns lsass.exe and services.exe; services.exe spawns all Windows services. Any deviation from these expected relationships is suspicious. svchost.exe must always have services.exe as its parent — if cmd.exe has spawned svchost.exe, that's immediate IOC.
3
Hidden Process Detection — Rootkits Can't Hide from psscan
Compare windows.pslist (which reads the kernel's doubly-linked list of EPROCESS structures) against windows.psscan (which scans raw memory for EPROCESS signatures regardless of list membership). Rootkits hide processes by unlinking them from the pslist — but the EPROCESS structure still exists in memory and psscan finds it. Any process appearing in psscan but not pslist is definitively hidden by a rootkit.
4
Injected Code Detection via malfind
Run windows.malfind to find memory regions that are executable, readable, and writable (PAGE_EXECUTE_READWRITE) and contain a PE header. Legitimate code is loaded from disk as executable-but-not-writable; writable executable memory is a strong indicator of injected code. The combination of RWX protection plus MZ header in a memory region that isn't mapped from a legitimate DLL is extremely suspicious.
5
Network Connections — Follow the C2
Run windows.netstat to see all current and recently-closed network connections. Look for ESTABLISHED connections to unusual external IPs (especially from processes that shouldn't be communicating externally — svchost.exe connecting to Cloudflare on port 443 is normal; svchost.exe connecting to a residential IP on port 8443 is not). Look for LISTENING ports — a legitimate process that has suddenly opened a listening port may indicate a reverse shell handler or implant waiting for connections.
6
DLL Analysis for Suspicious Processes
For any suspicious process identified in previous steps, run windows.dlllist --pid [PID] to see all loaded DLLs. Legitimate processes load DLLs from system paths (C:\Windows\System32, C:\Program Files). Any DLL loading from Temp directories, AppData, or user home directories is suspicious. Unsigned DLLs are increasingly rare in modern Windows (nearly all Microsoft DLLs are signed). A DLL in System32 whose path looks slightly off (double backslash, Unicode tricks) is a masquerading indicator.
7
Credential Extraction for Scope Assessment
Run windows.hashdump to extract NTLM hashes from LSASS memory. The forensic value here is scope assessment: if the attacker has SYSTEM or Administrator access (as evidenced by successful hashdump), they almost certainly have credentials that enable lateral movement. The hashes themselves can be submitted to threat intelligence services or cracked offline to determine which account passwords were exposed, which determines the scope of credential reset required.

Windows Forensics

Execution Artifacts — The Forensic Trinity

Prefetch

Windows Prefetch records execution history to speed up future launches. Each .pf file contains the executable name, a hash of the full path, the last 8 run times, and a list of files accessed during the first 10 seconds of execution. Prefetch survives even after the binary is deleted — so if an attacker executed a tool and then deleted it, Prefetch proves the execution happened. Prefetch is enabled by default on workstations but disabled by default on servers (servers disable it for performance reasons, which means you may not have this artifact in server incidents).

Shimcache (AppCompatCache)

Shimcache records executables that Windows has encountered — not necessarily executed, but seen. This is an important distinction: Shimcache presence proves a file existed on the system and was evaluated by the Application Compatibility engine, but it doesn't prove execution the way Prefetch does. Shimcache entries include the file path, size, and last modification timestamp. The list is stored in the registry (HKLM\System\CurrentControlSet\Control\Session Manager\AppCompatCache) and must be parsed with AppCompatCacheParser.exe. A key use case: Shimcache can prove a tool was present on a system even if no Prefetch entry exists (servers have Prefetch disabled; Shimcache is always enabled).

Amcache

Amcache (C:\Windows\AppCompat\Programs\Amcache.hve) stores SHA-1 hashes of executed executables. This is one of the most powerful pivot points in Windows forensics: you can extract hashes from Amcache and submit them directly to threat intelligence platforms (VirusTotal, MISP) to determine if known malware was executed. Even if the file has been deleted and file carving failed, if the SHA-1 hash in Amcache matches a known malware family, you have definitively proven that malicious code executed on this system.

Auditd Log Analysis

Linux Audit daemon (auditd) provides kernel-level visibility when configured with appropriate rules. The key advantage of auditd over standard logging is the auid field — the Audit User ID. When a user logs in, the kernel assigns their UID as the auid for that session. If they then use sudo to become root, or su to become another user, the auid does not change — it persistently reflects the original authenticated user. This makes auditd uniquely powerful for tracing actions back to the originating human even through multiple privilege escalation steps.

Every confirmed event yields multiple IOCs. From the email: the sender domain, the attachment filename, the attachment hash. The Pivot Methodology

Pivoting is the practice of using a confirmed IOC to search for additional evidence across all available data sources. When you confirm a malicious IP address, you immediately search for that IP in firewall logs (all systems communicating with it, not just the initially identified victim), DNS logs (all systems that resolved the associated domain), proxy logs (all HTTP/S traffic), endpoint logs (all systems where that IP appears in network connection events), and email logs (any email containing that IP or domain). A single confirmed IOC from one system can expand the investigation scope to a dozen additional compromised systems.

Network Forensics

Network captures at the perimeter are outside the attacker's control. Unlike endpoint logs — which an attacker can clear, manipulate, or evade — network data captured by infrastructure the attacker doesn't touch is uniquely reliable forensic evidence.

PCAP Analysis Methodology

The most common mistake in PCAP analysis is opening Wireshark and starting to read packets from the beginning. A 10-gigabyte PCAP file contains millions of packets. Reading them sequentially is not analysis — it's data entry. The correct approach is statistical: start with aggregate statistics, identify anomalous flows, follow specific streams, then extract files. This moves from wide to narrow, spending detailed analysis time on the packets that actually matter.

1
Statistics First — Identify Anomalous Flows
Use tshark to generate per-destination statistics: total connections, total bytes, unique ports. Anything in the top 10 by connection count or bytes transferred deserves scrutiny. Known CDN and cloud provider IPs are expected — unknown IPs in residential ranges or on unusual ports are immediate investigation targets.
2
Identify Anomalous Flows — Apply IOCs
Search the PCAP for known bad IPs, domains, and file hashes using tshark filters. Also apply behavioral filters: DNS queries with unusually long subdomains (DNS tunneling), HTTP connections with non-standard User-Agents, connections to external IPs on internal-only ports like 445 (SMB) or 3389 (RDP) indicating lateral movement staging.
3
Follow Streams — Reconstruct Sessions
For identified suspicious flows, follow the TCP/UDP stream to see the full conversation. In Wireshark: right-click any packet in the flow → Follow → TCP Stream. For TLS-encrypted traffic, stream following shows the encrypted bytes — you need the server private key or a pre-master secret log to decrypt. In many enterprise environments, TLS inspection proxies provide decrypted PCAPs.
4
Extract Files — Recover Transferred Data
Tshark and NetworkMiner can extract files transferred over HTTP, SMB, SMTP, and FTP from PCAP captures. Extracted files can be hashed and submitted to threat intelligence, or analyzed directly for malware characteristics. Files extracted from network captures don't have filesystem timestamps — but the PCAP timestamp of the network transfer serves as strong evidence of when the file was accessed.

C2 Beaconing Detection — Statistical Analysis

C2 beacons are connections made at regular intervals by malware to its command-and-control server. The regularity is their weakness — human browsing behavior is random and irregular, but automated beaconing is consistent. The statistical signature is a low standard deviation in inter-connection intervals (the time between each connection). A standard deviation under 30 seconds across 20+ connections in 24 hours is suspicious. Under 10 seconds is almost certainly automated. Under 3 seconds is definitively a beacon.

DNS Tunneling Detection

DNS tunneling encodes data inside DNS query subdomain labels, exploiting the fact that most organizations allow outbound DNS traffic without inspection. An attacker encodes commands or data as base64/hex subdomains (aGVsbG8gd29ybGQ.attacker.com), queries them against a DNS server they control, and the attacker's DNS server reads the encoded data. Responses encode the C2 command back in TXT or A records. Detection is based on subdomain length (legitimate domains rarely have subdomains over 30 characters) and query frequency (tunneling generates many more DNS queries than normal browsing).

Malware Forensics

Dissecting hostile code to understand what it did, how it works, and how far it spread.

Every malware investigation starts with the same problem: you have a file (or a memory region, or a network stream) and you need to answer three questions before anything else. First, what kind of thing is this — an executable, a script, a document dropper, a shellcode loader? Second, is this a known threat or something new? Third, what does it do? The static analysis pipeline answers all three without executing a single instruction.

🧠
Analyst Mindset

Never trust file extensions. An attacker who names a shellcode loader report.pdf is counting on you to double-click it. Your first action with any suspicious file is to identify its actual type via magic bytes. Extension-based identification is for users. Magic bytes are for analysts.

Static Analysis Pipeline

Static analysis means examining the file without executing it. This is always your first step — it gives you capability indicators without the risk of detonating the payload in an uncontrolled environment. The pipeline has five stages, each informing the next: type identification, hash lookup, strings extraction, PE header analysis, and entropy calculation. Work through them in order.

Dynamic Analysis

Dynamic analysis means executing the malware in a controlled environment and observing its behavior. This tells you what the malware actually does (as opposed to what you can infer statically), but it requires careful isolation so the detonation doesn't affect production systems or the broader network. The environment requirements are non-negotiable: an isolated virtual machine with a clean snapshot taken before execution, no network access to the production environment (use a simulated internet environment like INetSim or FakeNet-NG), and monitoring tools pre-installed and running before detonation.

During a dynamic analysis session, you observe behavior across four dimensions. File system changes: what files are created, modified, or deleted (use Process Monitor with a file system filter). Registry modifications: what keys are created or modified for persistence (Process Monitor with registry filter). Network connections: what connections are attempted, what protocols are used, what data is sent (Wireshark or Fakenet-NG for capture and simulation). Process activity: what child processes are spawned, what processes are injected into (Process Monitor process filter + Process Hacker for live process inspection).

Sandbox Evasion is Real

Sophisticated malware detects sandbox environments and behaves differently — or does nothing at all. Common detection techniques include checking for VM artifacts (VMware registry keys, VirtualBox drivers, unusual hardware), timing attacks (sandboxes have short execution windows, so malware sleeps past them), user interaction requirements (checking for mouse movement or window activity), and checking for analyst tools (Process Monitor, Wireshark, x64dbg). If your sandbox shows no malicious behavior, treat the sample as evasion-capable and proceed with static analysis and manual reverse engineering rather than assuming it's benign.

Anti-Analysis and Evasion Techniques

Modern malware doesn't just do its job — it actively tries to avoid being analyzed. Understanding evasion techniques is essential because an analyst who doesn't recognize evasion in progress will waste hours on a dead end, or worse, conclude the sample is benign when it's actually waiting for the right conditions to detonate.

01
VM and Sandbox Detection

Malware checks for evidence of virtualization by querying VMware/VirtualBox registry keys (HKLM\SOFTWARE\VMware, Inc.), checking for VM-specific processes (vmtoolsd.exe, vboxservice.exe), inspecting CPUID results for hypervisor bit, and checking if the MAC address belongs to a known virtualization vendor (VMware: 00:0C:29, VirtualBox: 08:00:27). Detection approach: use bare-metal analysis environments for high-value samples, or configure VMs to spoof hardware identifiers and suppress VM-specific processes. The presence of anti-VM code in a sample is itself a strong indicator of sophisticated, intentionally written malware.

02
Sleep-Based Sandbox Evasion

Automated sandbox environments have limited execution windows — typically 2-5 minutes. Malware exploits this by calling Sleep() for extended periods (30+ minutes) before executing its payload, causing the sandbox to report no malicious behavior and the sample to be classified as clean. Some variants check the actual elapsed time after sleeping — sandboxes often accelerate time skipping — to detect time manipulation. Detection approach: patch the sleep function in debugger sessions to return immediately (NOP the call or modify the delay parameter). During static analysis, strings containing large numeric arguments to Sleep() functions are a tell.

03
Parent Process Spoofing

Windows allows a process to specify its parent process ID at creation time, decoupling the reported parent from the actual creator. Malware uses UpdateProcThreadAttribute(PROC_THREAD_ATTRIBUTE_PARENT_PROCESS) to make malicious cmd.exe appear as a child of explorer.exe instead of the actual malware process, bypassing parent-child relationship detection rules. Forensic detection: cross-reference the reported parent PID with Sysmon Event 1 (ProcessCreate) — if the reported parent PID doesn't match the Sysmon-recorded ParentProcessId, spoofing occurred. Memory forensics with Volatility's windows.pstree shows both the reported and real process relationships.

04
Process Hollowing

Process hollowing starts a legitimate process (svchost.exe, explorer.exe) in a suspended state, unmaps its legitimate code using NtUnmapViewOfSection, writes malicious code into the now-empty address space, and resumes execution. From Task Manager and most security tools, the process appears legitimate — correct name, correct parent, correct path. The malicious code runs under the trusted process's identity and network connections appear to originate from a legitimate system process. Forensic detection: Volatility's windows.malfind identifies the PAGE_EXECUTE_READWRITE memory regions where the injected code lives, and the MZ header confirms a PE file was injected. The VAD (Virtual Address Descriptor) tree of the hollowed process will show executable memory regions that don't correspond to mapped DLLs.

05
Reflective DLL Injection

Standard DLL injection loads a DLL from disk, leaving an on-disk artifact and a Windows loader event that Sysmon records. Reflective DLL injection loads a specially crafted DLL from memory only — the DLL contains its own loader routine that resolves imports and fixes relocations without calling the Windows loader. The result is a DLL running in memory that never touches disk and appears in no loaded module list. Forensic detection: Volatility's windows.malfind shows the executable memory region, and windows.dlllist for the target process shows fewer DLLs than expected given its functionality. The injected region's memory protections (PAGE_EXECUTE_READWRITE transitioning to PAGE_EXECUTE_READ after unpacking) are forensically significant.

Shannon Entropy Analysis

Shannon entropy is a measure of information density in data, borrowed from information theory. The intuitive explanation: if every byte in a file is the same value (say, all zeros), entropy is 0 — perfectly predictable, no information. If every byte is random, entropy approaches 8.0 — maximum unpredictability. Normal files (text, executables, images) have entropy between 3.0 and 7.0. Encrypted or compressed data has entropy above 7.0 — it looks random because good encryption makes ciphertext indistinguishable from random bytes.

The forensic application: legitimate program files have characteristic entropy profiles. An executable with a .packed section at entropy 7.9 contains encrypted payload. A text configuration file with entropy 7.6 contains encrypted C2 configuration. A file named report.docx with entropy 7.8 is not a Word document — it's an encrypted payload with a misleading extension. Entropy scanning is particularly powerful for finding staged payloads that haven't been executed yet.

Timestomping Forensic Detection

Timestomping is the act of modifying file timestamps to make malware appear old (predating the investigation window) or to make it blend in with legitimate files of similar age. Attackers use tools like Metasploit's timestomp module or custom code that calls SetFileTime(). The key vulnerability in this technique: it only modifies the $STANDARD_INFORMATION attribute timestamps visible to Windows Explorer and most tools. The $FILE_NAME attribute, which is updated by the NTFS filesystem kernel and extremely difficult to fake without specialized tools, retains the original timestamps. Comparing these two sets reveals the discrepancy.

Statistical Anomaly Detection in Logs

Threshold-based detection (alert if event count exceeds X) fails against low-and-slow attacks and against attackers who know your thresholds. Peer group analysis is superior: instead of comparing an entity's behavior to an absolute threshold, compare it to the behavior of its peer group over the baseline period. A domain admin running PowerShell is expected behavior. A finance clerk running PowerShell for the first time in 30 days is an anomaly — the same event, the same threshold, completely different risk profiles.

powershell

Playbook 2: Malware Infection

01
Identify Patient Zero

Patient zero is the first system infected — every other infected system is downstream from it. Finding patient zero answers where the infection entered and when, which constrains the investigation window. Check your email gateway logs for the first delivery of the malware hash or C2 domain. Check your proxy logs for the first HTTP request to the C2 IP. Check Sysmon Event 11 (FileCreate) across your SIEM for the first instance of the malware's filename being created on disk. The system with the oldest timestamp for any of these indicators is patient zero. If you don't have centralized logging, check Last-Write timestamps on the malware file itself via $FILE_NAME timestamps to establish when it was first placed on each system.

02
Capture Memory Before Any Other Action

On every infected system, memory capture is the first action before containment, before remediation, before shutdown. This is non-negotiable. Memory contains the unpacked malware payload (even if the on-disk file is packed), active C2 network connections and the protocols being used, any credentials that have been accessed or cached, injected code in legitimate processes, and runtime configuration including the C2 address if the malware resolves it dynamically. Once you shut down the system or run remediation tools, all of this is gone. The five minutes it takes to run WinPmem is always worth it. Capture to a separate drive or network share, hash immediately, document in chain of custody.

03
Map ALL Persistence Mechanisms

This step is where most investigations fail. Analysts find one persistence mechanism, remove it, declare remediation complete, and the malware is back the next day via the second persistence mechanism they didn't find. Professional threat actors almost always install multiple persistence mechanisms — typically a primary (scheduled task or service) and a backup (WMI subscription or registry Run key) with different triggers. Your persistence hunt must cover all categories simultaneously: Run keys (HKLM and HKCU), Scheduled Tasks (schtasks /query /fo LIST /v), Services (sc query type= all state= all), WMI subscriptions (Get-WMIObject -Namespace root/subscription -Class __EventFilter), startup folders, browser extensions, and Office templates. Document every finding before removing any of them — removal order can affect your ability to document the others.

04
Determine Scope via Lateral Movement Analysis

After mapping persistence on patient zero, determine how far the infection spread. Lateral movement leaves specific evidence: Sysmon Event 3 (NetworkConnect) from the infected host to internal IPs on SMB (445), WMI (135), WinRM (5985/5986), and RDP (3389) ports. Windows Security Event 4624 with LogonType 3 (network) or LogonType 10 (remote interactive) on other systems with the patient zero IP as the source address. Sysmon Event 1 (ProcessCreate) on remote systems showing processes spawned by wmiprvse.exe or services.exe with unusual command lines — these are the hallmarks of remote execution. Build a lateral movement graph: which systems were accessed from which source, in what order, using what credentials.

05
Assess Damage: Credential Exposure and Data Access

The final question before remediation is: what did the attacker do while they were in? Credential exposure assessment: check for LSASS access (Sysmon Event 10 with TargetImage lsass.exe, GrantedAccess 0x1010 or 0x1410), Kerberoasting artifacts (Event 4769 with TicketEncryptionType 0x17), DCSync activity (Event 4662 on domain controllers), or password database access. Data access assessment: Event 4663 (file access) with sensitive directory paths, large outbound transfers in Zeek/proxy logs, USB write activity, cloud storage uploads (proxy logs for OneDrive, Dropbox, SharePoint large uploads). The damage assessment defines the breach notification obligation and determines the scope of credential rotation required. If LSASS was accessed, all credentials on all systems that user account could reach should be rotated.

🔥

Before collecting a single byte of evidence in an insider threat case, engage Legal and HR. Chain of custody must be maintained from the first moment, because evidence collected improperly may be inadmissible and could expose the organization to counterclaims. The three-phase methodology below is designed with legal defensibility as an explicit requirement — not an afterthought.

The insider threat methodology has three phases that must all be completed before reaching a conclusion. Phase one establishes the anomaly statistically — proving that the behavior is genuinely unusual for this user compared to their own baseline and their peer group. Phase two establishes intent indicators — evidence that the behavior was purposeful, not accidental. Phase three establishes the exfiltration path — how the data left (if it did). All three are required. An anomaly without intent evidence is just strange behavior. Intent evidence without an exfiltration path is concerning but may be preliminary. Only all three together support a definitive conclusion.

Chapter 12

Automation in Digital Forensics

Eliminating mechanical work so human expertise can focus on what machines cannot do.

The automation question in DFIR is not "should we automate?" — the answer is obviously yes. The real question is "what should we automate, and where does automation create false confidence?" Automation is a force multiplier when applied to deterministic, high-volume, well-understood tasks: collecting the same 40 artifact types from 1,000 endpoints simultaneously, correlating a known IOC against terabytes of logs, parsing MFT records. Automation fails — sometimes catastrophically — when applied to tasks requiring judgment, context, or novel pattern recognition. The goal of this chapter is to give you a clear model for which is which.

🧠
The Framing That Matters

Think of automation as handling layers 1-4 of a five-layer analysis pipeline: collection, parsing, IOC correlation, and triage scoring. Human expertise handles layer 5: interpretation, hypothesis formation, and conclusions. Automation without human layer-5 analysis produces alert queues nobody can act on. Human analysis without automation layers 1-4 produces analysts who spend 80% of their time on mechanical work instead of thinking. Both alone are insufficient.

Automation Pipeline Architecture

01
Collection — KAPE and Velociraptor

Artifact collection is the most automatable layer. The target is unambiguous (collect these specific files and registry keys), the process is deterministic (copy them without modification), and the scale benefit is enormous (doing it on 1,000 endpoints instead of 1 takes the same human effort). KAPE handles targeted collection from a single offline image or live system. Velociraptor handles fleet-wide collection across an enterprise simultaneously. Both should be part of every DFIR program.

02
Parsing — Plaso and Tool Normalization

Raw artifacts (binary MFT records, Windows event log .evtx files, SQLite browser databases) must be parsed into structured, searchable formats. Plaso/log2timeline normalizes hundreds of artifact formats into a single timeline. Eric Zimmerman's tools (MFTECmd, PECmd, AmcacheParser) parse individual artifact types into CSVs. This layer transforms binary forensic artifacts into searchable data — still entirely automatable because the format of each artifact is well-documented.

03
IOC Correlation — Automated Sweep

Once artifacts are parsed and normalized, known indicators of compromise (IP addresses, domain names, file hashes, registry paths) can be swept across the data automatically. This is deterministic matching: known bad hash found in Amcache → flag it. Known C2 IP found in proxy logs → flag it. This layer should run automatically on every new collection. High match rates indicate known malware families. Zero matches don't mean clean — they mean the attacker used novel IOCs.

04
Triage and Scoring — Automated Prioritization

Not all findings are equal. Automated triage scores findings based on severity, recency, and corroboration (a finding supported by three independent evidence sources scores higher than one supported by one). This layer produces a prioritized work queue for human analysts. The scoring algorithm is an explicit policy decision that should be reviewed periodically — what your organization treats as critical may differ from another's. The triage score is input to human analysis, not a replacement for it.

05
Human Analysis — The Irreplaceable Layer

Human analysts take the prioritized, corroborated, scored findings from layer 4 and do what automation cannot: form hypotheses about what the attacker was trying to accomplish, recognize patterns that don't match any known signature, apply organizational context (this server holds our most sensitive IP), make judgment calls about what to investigate next, and communicate findings to stakeholders in terms they understand. This is the highest-value layer and it requires the most experienced people. Automation's goal is to maximize the time experienced analysts spend here.

KAPE: Targeted Artifact Collection

KAPE (Kroll Artifact Parser and Extractor) is the standard tool for rapid, targeted artifact collection in enterprise DFIR. It operates in two phases: collection (gathering specific files using Target definitions) and processing (parsing collected artifacts using Module definitions). The SANS Triage collection target (!SANS_Triage) represents the consensus on the minimum artifact set required for a complete investigation — it was assembled by the same analysts who wrote most of the forensic guidance you've read in this handbook.

Where Automation Must NOT Replace Human Judgment

Every capability boundary in this list represents a category of failure that has occurred in real incidents when organizations over-relied on automation. Understanding these boundaries isn't academic — it directly affects how you design your SOC and what you communicate to executives about the limits of your automated tooling.

Domain Why Automation Fails Here What Human Judgment Provides
Attribution Algorithms can correlate TTPs, infrastructure, and code similarities. They cannot reason about intent, geopolitical context, deliberate false flag operations, or shared tool sets between unrelated actors. Analysts with threat intelligence context who understand why an actor would target this organization, with these TTPs, at this time. False flag attacks specifically exploit automated attribution.
Final Incident Conclusion Conclusions carry legal, regulatory, and organizational consequences. Whether to notify regulators, which customers were affected, whether to involve law enforcement — these require weighing factors automation cannot access: legal risk appetite, regulatory relationships, insurance obligations. Senior analysts, legal, and executive leadership who can weigh organizational context, regulatory requirements, and risk tolerance against the technical findings.
Novel Attack Detection Automation detects the known — patterns it has been trained on or rules that were written for known attack types. The most dangerous attacks are the ones no rule anticipated. A truly novel technique produces no alerts. Experienced analysts who can recognize that something is wrong even when they can't immediately articulate why — pattern recognition built from investigating hundreds of incidents rather than matching against a ruleset.
Hypothesis Formation Automation can generate alerts. It cannot ask "given what we know about this organization's business, what would a financially motivated attacker prioritize?" or "why would an attacker lateral-move TO this specific server rather than away from it?" Analysts who understand the organization's business context, the value of different data assets, and the attacker's likely objectives — enabling purpose-directed investigation rather than random artifact collection.
Evidence in Legal Context Automated findings must survive adversarial challenge. Defense attorneys will attack collection methodology, tool reliability, chain of custody, and interpretation. Automation cannot explain its reasoning to a court or respond to cross-examination. Human analysts who understand what they collected, how they collected it, why specific tools were chosen, and what the evidence means in plain language that a judge and jury can evaluate.
Unusual Attack Patterns An attacker who achieves their goal entirely through legitimate system administration tools — using only commands that administrators also run — generates no malicious-specific alerts. Automation with no signature to match finds nothing. Analysts who notice that an administrator performed 300 file copies over a weekend when no change window was scheduled, or that a read-only service account suddenly started querying active directory — contextual anomalies that rules cannot anticipate.

Pre-Automation Checklist

Before building any automation into your DFIR workflow, ask these seven questions. If you can't answer all of them, the automation isn't ready:

1. What specific question does this automation answer? — If you can't state it in one sentence, it's not well-defined enough to automate.

2. What is the false positive rate, and is it acceptable? — Automation that fires 500 times per day trains analysts to ignore it. Know your rate before deploying.

3. What is the false negative rate? — What attacks will this miss, and what is the consequence of missing them?

4. What human action does a positive result require? — If there's no defined response procedure, don't build the alert.

5. Is the output legally defensible? — Can you explain exactly how the automation reached its conclusion, with sufficient detail to survive a court challenge?

6. Who owns the maintenance? — Automation decays as the environment changes. Who will update it when it starts missing or over-alerting?

7. What does it cost when it fails? — Automation that silently fails is worse than no automation. Define failure modes and build monitoring for them.

The Human Analyst's Role in an Automated World

As automation handles mechanical work, analyst value shifts — and this is a good thing for analysts who understand it. The question "can I run Volatility and read the output?" is increasingly answerable by automation. The question "what does it mean that this financially motivated threat actor chose to target specifically our M&A data three weeks before our acquisition announcement closes?" is not. The analysts who thrive in an automated DFIR environment are those who develop the capabilities that automation cannot replicate.

Creative hypothesis generation is the first. Given a set of confirmed findings, what are the five possible explanations — including the uncomfortable ones that implicate insiders or systemic security failures? An analyst who only follows the evidence to the most obvious conclusion will miss the 20% of cases where the obvious conclusion is wrong. Novel pattern recognition is the second. Every experienced analyst has a mental library of "something is off here" moments that preceded major findings — the server that shouldn't be making DNS queries, the admin account that logged in from two countries in four hours, the process tree that's almost right but not quite. This intuition is built from hundreds of investigations and cannot be replicated by a rules engine.

Organizational context is the third capability. An analyst who knows that the Marketing database contains a confidential M&A pipeline, that the CFO's assistant has unusual access to treasury systems because she inherited a former treasurer's permissions, and that the IT team runs weekly scripts that look identical to LOLBin abuse — this analyst will draw correct conclusions from the same evidence that would mislead an analyst without that context. The fourth is judgment under uncertainty. Real investigations rarely have clean evidence. They have partial data, conflicting timestamps, missing logs, and time pressure. The ability to make defensible decisions with incomplete information, document the uncertainty honestly, and communicate it clearly to non-technical stakeholders is a human skill that automation cannot replicate and that organizations cannot function without.

The Final Principle

Automation is a shovel. An experienced analyst with a shovel finds buried evidence faster than an experienced analyst without one. But a shovel without an experienced analyst just digs random holes. Build automation to eliminate mechanical work. Invest in analysts to do the work only humans can do. The organizations that get this balance right respond faster, miss less, and make fewer mistakes when it matters most.
